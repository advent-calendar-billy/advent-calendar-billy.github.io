Boston
All Saints Way — A narrow alley off Hanover Street in the North End where local resident Peter Baldassari has, over decades, covered the walls with hundreds of saint cards, statues, and candles. It's behind a gate but visible through it. Clue potential: count something, or reference a specific saint.
The Skinny House — 44 Hull Street, directly across from Copp's Hill Burying Ground. About 10 feet wide at its widest. Local legend: two brothers inherited the lot; one built a normal house while the other was off at war, so the returning veteran built this sliver to spite him by blocking his light. Clue: the number on the door, or the year.
Ether Dome — Surgical amphitheater at Mass General where ether was first publicly demonstrated as anesthesia in October 1846. Still has the original tiered seating and an Egyptian mummy in the corner (Padihershef). Generally open to the public when not in use, but worth confirming hours.
Mapparium — Inside the Mary Baker Eddy Library in Back Bay. A three-story stained-glass globe from 1935 that you walk through on a bridge. The acoustics turn it into a whisper gallery — sound bounces strangely. Ticketed entry, so factor that into your hunt logistics.
Boston Athenaeum — One of the oldest independent libraries in the US (1807), on Beacon Street next to the State House. Beautiful reading rooms, a private feel, and yes — a book bound in human skin in their collection. Members-only for most of it but the entrance area and some tours are accessible.
Make Way for Ducklings — Bronze ducklings in the Public Garden, from Robert McCloskey's book. Eight ducklings plus Mrs. Mallard. Clue: their names (Jack, Kack, Lack, Mack, Nack, Ouack, Pack, Quack) are perfect for a puzzle.
Chinatown Park dragon — On the Rose Kennedy Greenway end of Chinatown. A long dragon-themed walkway with a serpentine play structure and Chinese zodiac elements embedded in the paving.
Holocaust Memorial — Six 54-foot glass towers on the Greenway near Faneuil Hall, etched with six million numbers representing victims. Steam rises from grates beneath. Sobering, so consider tone — might be better as a respectful pass-through than a clue location.
Brattle Book Shop — Downtown Crossing, one of America's oldest antiquarian bookstores (1825). The outdoor bargain lot has a giant mural of literary figures on the brick wall next to it. Easy clue: identify an author from the mural.
Copp's Hill Burying Ground — Second-oldest cemetery in Boston (1659). Mather family tomb is here. British soldiers used it as an artillery position during the siege of Boston and you can see musket-ball damage on some headstones. Epitaphs make excellent clues.
Somerville
Old Powder House — Stone tower in Nathan Tufts Park near Tufts. Originally a 1700s windmill, later a colonial gunpowder magazine. The British raid on it in September 1774 (the "Powder Alarm") was a dress rehearsal for Lexington and Concord. Tucked oddly inside a traffic rotary.
Prospect Hill Tower — Castle-like stone tower on Prospect Hill where Washington raised the Grand Union Flag on January 1, 1776 — arguably the first American flag. The hill has a great skyline view. Tower interior access is limited (occasionally open for events).
Davis Square — The bronze statues include "The Bagman" sitting on a bench. Embedded in sidewalks around the square are poems and quotes — easy raw material for clues that require players to actually read what's underfoot.
Bow Market — Converted brick courtyard off Somerville Ave with ~30 small vendors: ice cream, natural wine, vintage clothes, ceramics. Great anchor for a hunt — players can rest, eat, and you could partner with a vendor to host a code or clue.
Museum of Modern Renaissance — A house on College Ave painted top to bottom by Russian artists Nicholas Shaplyko and Ekaterina Sorokina with mythological imagery. Private home, exterior only — be respectful, no entering. Just a "find this and photograph a specific detail" stop.
Somerville Community Path — Greenway with rotating murals, connects to the Minuteman Bikeway. Good connective tissue between stops if you want a walking/biking hunt.
Cambridge
Mount Auburn Cemetery — First garden cemetery in America (1831), and still working. Longfellow, Winslow Homer, Mary Baker Eddy, Buckminster Fuller, Julia Ward Howe are all buried here. Climb Washington Tower for a view. Cryptic monuments and symbolism everywhere — a treasure hunter's dream, though pace it respectfully.
Stata Center — Frank Gehry's deconstructivist MIT building (2004). Looks like it's collapsing on purpose. Lots of weird angles and details to use as visual clues.
Glass Flowers — At the Harvard Museum of Natural History. About 4,300 glass botanical models made by Leopold and Rudolf Blaschka between 1887 and 1936. Indoor, ticketed — works better as a finale than a midpoint.
A pragmatic note: indoor/ticketed locations (Athenaeum, Mapparium, Glass Flowers, Ether Dome) complicate a hunt because of hours, fees, and crowd control. They work better as bonus stops or finale destinations than mid-hunt waypoints. The free outdoor spots are where the actual hunt should live.
